\subsection{Real-Graph Bandit on MovieLens-100K}\label{sec:exp-real}
%-----------------------------------------------------------------------------

This experiment evaluates Theorem~\ref{thm:main-graph} on a real-world graph in which the smoothness assumption $\langle\bm{\mu},L_G\bm{\mu}\rangle\le\varepsilon^2$ is not engineered to hold. The graph is the item--item adjusted-cosine similarity network of the MovieLens-100K rating dataset; the true mean of each arm is the corresponding movie's average user rating. Unlike the synthetic instances of Sections~\ref{sec:exp-graph}--\ref{sec:exp-kernel}, neither the gap structure nor the graph topology is engineered to favour the algorithm.

\paragraph{Instance.}
Arms are the $K=20$ most-rated movies in MovieLens-100K \citep{harper2015movielens}; restricting to the most-rated movies controls for sampling-noise heterogeneity in $\widehat\mu_i$ and avoids the long tail. Edge weights are constructed by computing the adjusted-cosine similarity between item rating vectors (mean-centring each item over its raters, then taking the cosine over the user vectors) and sparsifying via mutual top-$k$ neighbour union with $k=5$ \citep{sarwar2001item}; pairs with fewer than $5$ common raters are excluded. The resulting graph has $68$ edges (mean degree $6.8$), is fully connected, and exhibits Laplacian smoothness $\varepsilon=\sqrt{\langle\bm{\mu},L_G\bm{\mu}\rangle}\approx 3.4$ on the $1$--$5$ rating scale. The smallest suboptimality gap is $\Delta_{\min}\approx 0.07$ (between Schindler's List and Casablanca, both with $\widehat\mu\approx 4.46$).

\paragraph{Reward model.}
At each pull of arm $i$, the environment samples a reward uniformly with replacement from movie $i$'s observed user ratings, following the convention of \citet{zong2016cascading,wu2019contextual}. The per-arm rating standard deviations range from $0.88$ to $1.17$ (median $0.99$); the resulting reward stream is bounded in $\{1,2,3,4,5\}$ rather than Gaussian, so the algorithms operate under model misspecification with respect to their sub-Gaussian posterior assumption.

\paragraph{Algorithms.}
We compare four methods, all with $\delta=10^{-3}$ and $q=0.1$. The graph-aware methods are TS-Explore (Algorithm~\ref{algo:ts-explore-graph}) and GRUB \citep{thaker2022maximizing}, both swept over $\rho\in\{1,3,10,30,100,300,1000\}$. The graph-unaware references are Basic TS (the agreement-stopping rule on the empirical-mean estimator) and \emph{KL-LUCB} \citep{kalyanakrishnan2012pac}, an LUCB-style elimination algorithm that pulls the empirical-best and runner-up arms until their confidence intervals separate. Basic TS and KL-LUCB depend only on $\bm{\mu}$ and are run once with the same seeds, broadcast across $\rho$ for visual reference.

\paragraph{Results.}
Across $\rho\in[1,1000]$ all $560$ runs ($4$ algorithms $\times\,7$ values of $\rho$ $\times\,20$ seeds, with the two non-graph baselines broadcast) correctly identify the highest-mean-rated arm. The graph-unaware references occupy two horizontal regimes: Basic TS terminates in roughly $\mathrm{TBD}_{\text{Basic}}$ pulls (median over seeds), while KL-LUCB---which spends nearly all of its sample budget on the closest-competitor pair---terminates in $\mathrm{TBD}_{\text{KL-LUCB}}$ pulls. TS-Explore's stopping time decreases monotonically in $\rho$ across the swept range: at $\rho=1$ it is statistically indistinguishable from Basic TS (both estimators are dominated by their data terms), drops by an order of magnitude at $\rho=300$, and reaches its tested-range minimum of $\mathrm{TBD}_{\text{TS}}$ pulls at $\rho=1000$.

\paragraph{Discussion.}
Three observations qualify the empirical optimum at $\rho=1000$. First, the theorem-prescribed value $\rho^\star(\varepsilon)\approx 14$ obtained by substituting $\varepsilon=3.4$ into the bound of Theorem~\ref{thm:main-graph} corresponds to a regime where TS-Explore is statistically indistinguishable from Basic TS; the bound is therefore sufficient but not tight on this instance, and a practitioner cannot rely on the closed form for tuning. Second, the optimum lies at the upper boundary of the swept range, indicating that the regularization is operating in a regime in which all $K-1$ suboptimal arms become \emph{non-competitive} (in the sense of Theorem~\ref{thm:main-graph}'s competitive set), so the closed-form $H_{\mathrm{graph}}$ collapses to the maximum-gap term rather than benefiting from per-cluster pooling. Third, $\varepsilon\approx 3.4$ on a rating scale in $[1,5]$ is large by the standards of our synthetic experiments---the graph is only weakly smooth---so the regime in which graph regularization helps is narrower than for engineered instances.

\paragraph{Scope.}
We restrict the experiment to $K=20$ because at larger $K$ the smallest separation between top-rated movies collapses below the per-arm noise scale: the top-$50$ subset has $\Delta_{\min}\approx 0.02$ and the top-$100$ subset $\Delta_{\min}\approx 0.01$, so Basic TS would require $\Omega(10^7)$ samples per arm in worst case. At $K=20$ this is a real but mild restriction; an ablation across $K\in\{10,15,20\}$ and graph constructions $k_{\mathrm{nbr}}\in\{3,5,10,20\}$ (Appendix~\ref{app:movielens-ablations}) confirms the qualitative ordering TS-Explore $<$ Basic TS at the chosen $\rho$ is preserved across these settings.
